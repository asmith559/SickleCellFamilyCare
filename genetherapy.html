Gene Therapy for Sickle Cell Disease

Breakthrough treatments offering new hope for sickle cell patients

Gene Therapy for Sickle Cell Disease: What You Need to Know

According to Sickle Cell Disease Association of America

In December 2023, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved two groundbreaking gene therapies for sickle cell disease: exagamglogene autotemcel and lovotibeglogene autotemcel. If you're considering these new treatment options, you may have questions. Below, we provide key information to help you understand gene therapy and its implications.

These innovative treatments represent the most significant advancement in sickle cell disease treatment in decades, offering potential freedom from painful crises and other complications.

Is Gene Therapy a Cure for Sickle Cell Disease?

Gene therapy is considered a potentially curative treatment—but more long-term research is needed to confirm lifelong effectiveness. Studies show a significant reduction in pain episodes, yet the FDA currently requires 15 years of patient follow-up after treatment.

While the results so far are promising, scientists are cautiously optimistic as they continue to monitor patients for lasting effects and any potential long-term complications.

How Does Gene Therapy Work?

  1. Stem Cell Collection

    Doctors extract blood-forming stem cells from your body. Multiple sessions may be needed to collect enough cells.

  2. Laboratory Modification

    Exagamglogene autotemcel uses gene editing technology.

    Lovotibeglogene autotemcel uses gene addition technology.

    The goal is to produce red blood cells that do not sickle.

  3. Chemotherapy Preparation

    A conditioning regimen clears original bone-marrow cells to make room for the modified cells.

  4. Reinfusion of Modified Cells

    Modified cells are infused back into the body, followed by at least a month in hospital and ongoing clinic visits.

  5. Long-Term Follow-Up

    Regular monitoring for 15 years post-treatment to ensure effectiveness and detect any potential issues.

When Did Gene Therapy Become Available?

Gene therapy for sickle cell disease became available in early 2024, following FDA approval in December 2023. Medical centers began implementing these treatments gradually throughout 2024, with more centers expected to offer them in the coming years.

Who Is Eligible for Gene Therapy?

  • Approved for: People 12 years and older with Sickle Cell Disease (SS) and Sickle Beta Zero Thalassemia.
  • Not included: SC type and other less common variants.
  • Patients must be healthy enough to undergo the chemotherapy conditioning process.
  • Individual evaluation by specialized treatment centers is required.

What Are the Potential Side Effects?

  • Infertility or increased risk of secondary cancer due to chemotherapy
  • Temporary immune suppression during the treatment process
  • Hair loss from chemotherapy conditioning
  • Risk of infection during treatment
  • Unknown long-term effects (still being studied)

Where Can I Receive Gene Therapy?

Only a limited number of U.S. centers with specialized expertise currently offer these treatments. As of 2025, approximately 50 medical centers nationwide have the capability to administer gene therapy for sickle cell disease, with more centers being trained each year.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Gene Therapy Work for All Types of Sickle Cell Disease?

These therapies are designed to boost fetal hemoglobin (HbF) in all SCD types, though most clinical data focus on SS and S β⁰ thalassemia. Effectiveness may vary based on individual factors and disease severity.

Is This the First Use of Gene Therapy?

Exagamglogene autotemcel is the first FDA-approved CRISPR gene editing therapy. Gene addition technology has been used to treat other conditions like retinal degeneration, spinal muscular atrophy (SMA), beta-thalassemia, and more.

Is Gene Therapy Safe? How Do I Know If It's Right for Me?

You should weigh potential benefits against risks with your medical team based on your health history and personal needs. The decision involves considering your disease severity, quality of life, ability to manage current treatments, and your comfort with the uncertainties of a newer treatment.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • How long does the entire treatment process take from start to finish?
  • What is the time commitment for follow-up appointments?
  • Where is the nearest treatment center, and would I need to relocate temporarily?
  • What alternative treatments exist if I'm not eligible for gene therapy?
  • How will we manage fertility concerns before treatment?
  • What is your experience with gene therapy for sickle cell disease?
  • How will we know if the treatment is working?
  • What support services are available during and after treatment?
